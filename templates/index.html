What is the APV Calculator?

The Actuarial Present Value Calculator is a calculator that helps you value your life insurance plans. This allows you to decide between different life insurance plans offered by the insurers.

What is an Actuarual Present Value?

Life insurance is valued through what is called the Actuarial Present Value. In short, it is the expected value of the present value of a possible future cash payout(s). The higher the APV, the more valuable a plan is. Let's break down what this actually means.

Let's say you have a whole life insurance that will pay you $1M when you inevitably die, sounds good for your descendant right? Well not necessarily. When your descendants get this money matters, as we all experienced from the post pandemic inflation each year the purchasing power of our money decreases. That is why it is important to calculate the present value of future cashflows. The present value of a future $1M is how much of today's money can buy an equivalent amount of things*. Let's say you are 30 and just had your first child, you might expect to die 50 years later, and That $1M in 50 years time would only be worth $291k of today's money assuming a consistent 2.5% inflation! That is only 30% of the headline value! Scary right?

Well, other than just the adage that a dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow. We can't know when we will die. A whole life insurance might guarantee a payout, but when you that be? Hopefully its not tomorrow even if you get to avoid the payout's devaluation through inflation, but will it be in exactly 50 years? Maybe 60? Or in 20 due to an accident? Life is too unpredictable to say with any certainty. Furthermore, if you have a term life which terminates at 65 rather than a whole life which guarantees a payout, how can you be sure you will even see a single cent of that $1M?

This is where the expected value comes in. We can assume that our own mortality follows that of the population we are part of. This population's mortality can then tell us our probability of death at any given age through a life table. In simple terms, if we expect to die in a year with 1% chance, then that million dollar payout is worth $10k in a year before discounting to the present value. When we do this for every year the life plan is in effect, then we can get a proper APV and thus a proper value for our life insurance!

* We will be using inflation as our discount factor rather than the more usual risk free rate. As this is for the general consumer, inflation devaluing the payout is more pertinent.

How to compare different life insurance plans?

Establishing the value of a life insurance plan is just 1 part of the equation. A plan might be worth a lot but if you are paying through the nose for it then the trade off might not be worth it. A simple metric to be used for comparison is then the value to cost ratio.
